The Sonic Storehouse

This sound work inquires into the ambiguity of digital piracy in a speculative future. Depicting a dial-up unauthorised library of sound files, the project draws parallels between historical archival practice and digital piracy along a potential future trajectory.

The work draws from algorithmic methodology at multiple points and incorporates audio aesthetics that might otherwise be considered anachronistic for the future to remind the listener of the chronology of digital piracy – that the line of its future trajectory can be traced back to its origins in web 1.0 and even further, through the history of private libraries, archives and literal, physical file sharing throughout history.

The work implicitly and explicitly presents benefits and issues related to piracy, such as increasing accessibility and erasing artist credit, and positions the listener to be complicit in the act without prescriptively demanding they either embrace or renounce piracy themselves.

The sound design for this video was an opportunity for experimentation and exploration of synchretic and diegetic sound. Michel Chion’s Audio-Vision: Sound on Screen (1993) advises that when sonic and visual elements coincide at the same time, they are ‘melded’ together in the audience’s mind in 'synchresis', regardless of the rationality of the pairing. Many of the sounds used in the video are illogical or do not mirror reality, however they convey meaning seamlessly enough that their presence in the sound design goes unquestioned by audiences.

This video draws on such classic texts as "Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse" (Rothman, Ramsay & Persichetti, 2018) and "Merlin" (BBC TV series, 2008-2012) and recognises their mastery in applying both synchretic and diegetic sound techniques.
